Mechanics

Health and Injuries

The health mechanic is fundamental to keeping the character alive, with a system that monitors everything from small scratches to fatal injuries.
• Injuries: Scratches, cuts, bites, and fractures can occur during combat or exploration. Each type of injury has different consequences. Bites, for example, lead to inevitable zombification.
• Treatment: Using bandages, disinfectants, and medical kits is essential. Some more severe injuries require specific items, such as needles for sutures.
• Infection: Untreated open wounds can become infected, progressively decreasing health and, in cases of zombie infection, resulting in the character's death.
• Diseases: Colds, food poisoning, and other conditions can arise depending on the player's actions and environment.

Basic Needs

Managing the character's physical and psychological needs is vital for survival.
• Hunger: Eating perishable and non-perishable foods is essential. Cooking food improves its efficiency and avoids poisoning.
• Thirst: Water is a scarce resource as the public supply fails. Collecting and purifying water is crucial.
• Fatigue: Sleeping in safe places improves physical and mental performance. Long periods without rest reduce effectiveness in combat and tasks.
• Mental Health: Anxiety, panic, and depression can arise when witnessing traumatic events. Reading books, listening to music, or being in comfortable environments helps manage these conditions.

Stealth and Combat

Surviving in Project Zomboid often means avoiding combat, but knowing how to fight and protect yourself is important.
• Stealth: Crouching reduces noise and allows you to pass unnoticed by zombies. Planning safe routes is essential.
• Zombie Detection System: They react to visual and sound stimuli, such as footsteps or doors being opened.
• Melee Weapons: Tools like machetes and bats are common, effective, and silent but have limited durability.
• Firearms: Pistols, shotguns, and rifles are powerful but the noise attracts hordes. Learning to use them strategically is important.
• Escapes: Instead of fighting, it is often safer to run or hide, reducing the risk of injury.
